How to Start an LLC

LLC requirements vary state by state, and it’s important that you are well-versed in your specific state laws before proceeding. Generally, it comes down to five basic steps.

Create Your LLC Now

1.Name Your LLC

Naming your LLC is an important step in the process of creating your limited liability company. The name you choose must be unique and not already in use by another business. It is also important to make sure that the name you choose does not infringe on any trademarks or existing business names. It is recommended to conduct a thorough name search to ensure that your desired name is available for use. The name of your LLC should also accurately reflect the nature of your business and be easily recognizable and memorable to potential customers. Consider using keywords that are related to your industry or service, and keep the name short, simple and straightforward.

Search for a company name online.

Make sure your selected business name is available by conducting research. Google and social media research are important, but you should also conduct a business database search on the Secretary of State website.
Your LLC's name must be unique compared to that of other LLCs and cannot have been previously registered as a trademark. Federal and state trademarks are the two types to be aware of. Check your company name or logo at the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) to see if it hasn't already been federally trademarked.

Setting up a trademark

Once you’ve determined that it’s available to use, you have the option of registering your own trademark. A state trademark is less expensive and much less complicated to get; however, it does restrict your trademark benefits to the state it’s recognized in.
On the other hand, federal trademarks are more costly and can take longer to get, but you can use your trademark nationwide, and there is much more protection provided for your company. Federal trademarks also allow for the ® symbol, whereas state trademarks only allow TM (trademark) or SM (service mark). Trademarking your LLC can keep other businesses from using the same name or anything too similar.

Making a DBA Registration for Your LLC

A DBA name, or "doing business as," can also be added to your LLC. A DBA is just another name for your company, and it can be very helpful if your LLC provides a variety of goods and services. It might aid in establishing a distinction between their various business issues. In terms of naming an LLC, each state has its own rules. Certain words, such as those that are deemed profane or obscene or that might deceive people about the nature of the business, are frequently forbidden. Most states limit the use of certain words, including "bank" and its variants ("banking" and "banker"), "engineering," "insurance," and "savings." In some states, however, business owners who wish to use certain phrases must obtain a licence..

2. Choose a Registered Agent

In essence, a registered agent serves as a conduit between an LLC and the state in which it is registered. This third-party person or company serves as the company's point of contact and receives letters from the government, tax forms, legal documents, and notices of lawsuits.

As long as you have an actual street address in the state where your LLC is incorporated (P.O. boxes are not permitted), you can serve as your own registered agent; but, using an outside registered agent service has its advantages.

The additional burden that comes with being your own agent might be lessened and you are given more freedom and privacy. Using a registered agent service from a third party.

Appoint a Registered Agent

3. Submit the articles of incorporation or certificate of formation.

The Certificate of Formation, also known as the Articles of Organization, is a legal document that is filed with the relevant state government agency to form a limited liability company (LLC). This document serves as official proof of the creation of the LLC and outlines the basic information about the company, such as its name, registered agent, and purpose. The Certificate of Formation sets forth the rules and regulations for the company's internal management and operations and is used to determine the company's status as a separate legal entity from its owners, known as members. Additionally, the Certificate of Formation is a crucial step in the process of obtaining any necessary licenses or permits for the LLC to legally conduct business.

4. Get an Operating Agreement

The next step is to draught an operating agreement for an LLC. Only a few jurisdictions have an operating agreement requirement, yet it's an essential legal document for an LLC. An operating agreement often outlines the rules your firm will go by (akin to the corporate bylaws for a corporation), specifies LLC members, each member's share of ownership, how the company's profits will be distributed, the procedures for adding and removing members, and more. By having a written agreement in place, the members' personal liability protection is further strengthened in the event that someone sues them in court to demonstrate that the LLC isn't really a separate legal entity (so they can seize the members' personal assets rather than simply the business's). The operational agreement also explains how finances are managed.

5.Register for an EIN and review the tax regulations

To start a business, it is important to register for an EIN, also known as an Employer Identification Number. This number is used by the Internal Revenue Service to identify businesses for tax purposes. Once you have registered for an EIN, it is crucial to review the tax regulations to ensure that your business is in compliance with all federal and state tax laws. This includes understanding the tax obligations of your business such as paying taxes on income, sales, and payroll. Failure to comply with tax regulations can result in penalties and legal consequences, so it is essential to stay informed and proactive in your tax planning and preparation.
